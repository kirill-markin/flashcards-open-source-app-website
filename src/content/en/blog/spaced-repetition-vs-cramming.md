It is 9:15 on Sunday night. The exam starts at 9:00 Monday morning, and three chapters still have sticky notes saying “review.” In the **spaced repetition vs cramming** comparison, spaced retrieval is the better default for an exam and for anything you need afterwards. If the exam is tomorrow, targeted cramming can still make a small set of facts easier to access by morning. Expect less durable learning and less time to find gaps.

That answer leaves room for the deadline you actually have. Your plan depends on when the exam starts, what kind of answer it demands, and whether the knowledge still matters after the test.

## Spaced practice and massed practice are different study patterns

**Spaced practice**, also called **distributed practice**, separates learning across sessions. Strong exam preparation usually adds retrieval: study a formula on Monday, solve a problem from memory on Wednesday, then meet it inside a mixed set on Saturday.

Opening the same notes on three evenings is also distributed in time. It is still passive rereading, so familiarity with the page can hide weak recall. A closed book gives better evidence: can you produce the definition, distinction, or method before seeing it again? The guide to [active recall vs spaced repetition](/blog/active-recall-vs-spaced-repetition/) explains the difference between retrieval and scheduling.

**Massed practice** puts repetitions close together, often in one long session. Cramming might mean rereading a chapter twice, repeating the same quiz until the options look familiar, or cycling through a new deck several times in one evening.

Time spent is only part of the comparison. Two students can study for six hours. One spreads retrieval across several days; the other compresses the same work into one block.

## Choose the method by the retention horizon

The exam date matters. So does the day after it. Use this table to choose a study pattern, without treating it as a promise about results.

| Goal | Main approach | What to do |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Exam tomorrow | Targeted deadline triage | Test yourself, repair known misses and high-value facts, then practice the real exam format |
| Exam next week | Spaced retrieval with targeted repair | Use several short sessions, revisit weak material after delays, and mix in practice questions |
| Cumulative final or certification | Distributed retrieval across the full preparation window | Keep older topics returning while adding new material; use full-format practice regularly |
| Knowledge needed after the test | Ongoing spaced retrieval | Continue reviews after the exam and apply the knowledge in realistic tasks |

Tomorrow may leave only enough time for a targeted cram session. A week allows several retrieval attempts with real delays between them. For a cumulative final, earlier topics need to return while you learn the latest material. Professional, language, and technical knowledge usually deserve reviews after the score is published.

One universal `1-3-7` plan is too neat for all of those goals. Useful gaps vary with the intended retention interval, and topics start at different levels of familiarity. A practical [flashcard review schedule](/blog/how-often-should-you-review-flashcards/) should respond to the deadline and to actual recall.

## Why cramming feels so effective

Cramming gives fast feedback. Read a term, see it again thirty seconds later, and the answer arrives quickly. By the fourth pass, the page looks familiar and a same-session quiz feels easier.

[Soderstrom and Bjork’s review](https://doi.org/10.1177/1745691615569000) draws the important line between performance, which is visible during practice, and learning, which has to support later retention and transfer. Performance can improve while the underlying change remains fragile. Learners also tend to treat smooth performance during study as proof that learning has happened.

Closely repeated material feels fluent because the wording, context, and answer were just active. Delayed retrieval removes some of that support. Hesitation after a delay can feel discouraging, but it reveals more than another immediate pass.

[Roediger and Karpicke](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-9280.2006.01693.x) made this timing problem concrete with prose passages. Repeated study produced better recall on a final test after five minutes. Repeated free-recall testing produced better retention after two days or one week, even though repeated study raised confidence. The experiment compared study and retrieval schedules under controlled conditions. It did not test a complete night-before exam plan, FSRS, or a flashcard app.

## What the research supports

The broad result is well established for delayed retention: learning episodes spread over time usually outperform the same work massed together.

[Cepeda and colleagues’ 2006 meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16719566/) reviewed 317 experiments on verbal recall. It found that the gap between learning episodes and the delay before the final test work together. A schedule suited to a short retention goal may be wrong for a much longer one. The review supports spacing, without supplying one best calendar for every learner and subject.

[Dunlosky and colleagues](https://www.psychologicalscience.org/publications/journals/pspi/learning-techniques.html) evaluated ten learning techniques. They rated practice testing and distributed practice as high-utility approaches across a broad range of conditions. Used together, spacing determines when material returns and retrieval shows whether you can still produce it.

These sources do not show that cramming never works. They show why success during a massed session can overstate what will remain later. They also do not promise a grade, specify a universal number of hours, or show that a scheduler can repair unclear material. Accurate sources, useful prompts, understanding, and exam-format practice still matter.

## The narrow job cramming can do well

A targeted cram block can improve immediate accessibility when the deadline has arrived. Keep the scope small.

Good targets include:

- formulas you understand but cannot reproduce cleanly
- vocabulary or labels that the exam expects verbatim
- two similar concepts you keep swapping
- short rules, conditions, or exceptions
- mistakes already exposed by a practice set

Start from known misses. A closed-book quiz that exposes six weak items gives you a better cram list than a 90-page chapter marked “important.”

Cramming becomes less useful as the task expands. An unfamiliar proof, a clinical scenario, a long essay, or a multi-step calculation needs more than access to isolated facts. Repeating a worked solution can make that solution look obvious while leaving you unprepared to choose a method for a new problem.

Once the exam passes, rescued material needs later retrieval if you want to keep it. Put the important subset into a spaced plan after the deadline.

## A four-to-seven-days-left plan

With four to seven days remaining, you have time for genuine spacing. Avoid spending the first two days making a beautiful schedule or importing every paragraph into a deck.

1. **Map the exam from the actual specification.** List topics, question types, marks, and any formula or vocabulary requirements. Split them into secure, uncertain, and weak.
2. **Run a closed-book baseline.** Use a mixed practice set, blank-page recall, or short questions. Record wrong, incomplete, and slow answers. Do this before rereading.
3. **Repair weak material the same day.** Relearn the concept from the source, work one example, then test it without looking. Turn only small reusable facts, cues, and distinctions into cards.
4. **Bring weak topics back later.** Start each new session with retrieval from an earlier one. A one-day gap is workable this close to an exam. Give stable material more room and bring failures back sooner.
5. **Use mixed exam-format practice.** Each day should include questions that combine topics or require the real output: calculations, explanations, proofs, scenarios, or essays.
6. **Reduce new intake near the deadline.** On the final day, prioritize due reviews, known misses, and a manageable mixed set. New material must be important enough to displace something already in the plan.

This loop gives weak material several encounters while keeping the real exam task visible. The longer [FSRS exam study guide](/blog/how-to-study-for-an-exam-with-fsrs/) covers intake and review load. The [finals workflow](/blog/how-to-study-for-finals-with-flashcards/) helps when several courses compete for the same week.

## An exam-tomorrow rescue plan

When the exam is tomorrow, the work is deadline triage. Use the remaining hours to improve tomorrow’s performance. One evening cannot create weeks of distributed practice.

Use this order:

1. Check the exam scope and scoring. Remove low-value material that is unlikely to appear or carries few marks.
2. Do a short closed-book diagnostic. Use representative questions, not a comfortable reread.
3. Make three groups: secure, repairable tonight, and too large to rebuild. Spend most time on the middle group.
4. Study one small target, close the source, retrieve or solve it, check the answer, and repeat after another topic has intervened.
5. Practice at least a few questions in the actual exam format. Time one section if pacing is part of the assessment.
6. Finish with a short list of formulas, vocabulary, distinctions, and known misses. Stop early enough to protect a realistic sleep window and prepare what you need for the morning.

Skip the giant new deck and easy repetitions that only raise a completion number. Keep card ratings honest. If a card is next due after the exam, leave its schedule alone and put the few items that need another check tonight on a separate last-minute list.

After the exam, decide which rescued material still matters and return that subset to spaced retrieval.

## Flashcards cover only part of exam performance

Flashcards are useful for atomic material: facts, definitions, formulas, vocabulary, cues, short sequences, close distinctions, and known misses. They make retrieval easy to repeat and rate.

They are a poor substitute for producing the full answer an exam demands. Keep these activities in the plan:

- write essay outlines and complete timed responses
- solve calculations from a blank page
- construct proofs without copying the worked path
- answer case or clinical scenarios with mixed evidence
- complete full practice questions and timed sections

A card can ask when to use a formula. A problem set shows whether you can recognize that moment inside unfamiliar wording. A card can store an essay’s core distinction. Writing the essay shows whether you can build the argument under time pressure.

Use the tools in sequence. Let realistic questions expose gaps, store the small ones worth retaining, then return to realistic questions. [Flashcards vs practice tests](/blog/flashcards-vs-practice-tests/) goes deeper on that division of labour.

## How FSRS helps without studying for you

FSRS automates return timing for cards. The official [Free Spaced Repetition Scheduler project](https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition/free-spaced-repetition-scheduler) models a card’s difficulty, stability, and retrievability. After each attempt, your rating supplies the signal for the next scheduling decision.

In Flashcards Open Source App, the current scheduler is FSRS-6. The queue shows cards that are due, and reviews use four ratings:

- `Again` for failed recall
- `Hard` for a difficult but successful recall
- `Good` for an ordinary successful recall
- `Easy` for an effortless successful recall

The default desired retention is `0.90`. Raising it generally asks for more frequent reviews and creates more work; lowering it allows wider intervals and less work. The setting guides interval calculations. It does not guarantee that exactly 90% of a session will be correct. The [FSRS settings guide](/blog/fsrs-settings/) explains the trade-off.

The product uses pinned FSRS-6 weights rather than training personalized weights from your review history. Scheduler settings are forward-only: a change affects future reviews and does not rebuild existing due dates. Those boundaries are documented in the public [FSRS scheduling specification](https://github.com/kirill-markin/flashcards-open-source-app/blob/main/docs/fsrs-scheduling-logic.md).

FSRS removes calendar bookkeeping. It cannot fix a bad card, decide which chapters carry the most marks, or practice an essay for you. Near an exam, the due queue remains one part of the plan alongside targeted weak-area review and full-format questions.

## The decision checklist

Before choosing **cramming vs spaced repetition**, check five things:

- How many hours or days remain before the assessment?
- Will you need this knowledge next week, next term, or at work?
- Does the exam test small facts, applied decisions, or extended performance?
- Have you tested yourself without notes and identified the actual misses?
- Can the study plan create at least one delayed return to weak material?

Several days give you time to distribute retrieval. One evening calls for a narrow list of repairable, high-value items. Keep practice questions in either plan, and continue spaced reviews after the test for knowledge that still matters.

You can follow every step here with paper, a notes file, and practice questions. If you want FSRS to manage card dates, review the [Flashcards features](/features/) or [open the hosted app](https://app.flashcards-open-source-app.com). Choose the study pattern from the deadline and from how long the knowledge must last.
